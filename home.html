The bet

A Home that wasn't doing its job

Landbot's Home was a pass-through page — it didn't help new users start, and gave returning customers no reason to open it.

New users hit a blank wall: no clear path from sign-up to a first working bot, and no answer to "what should I build first?" Active customers had the opposite problem — they couldn't see how their bots were performing without digging into each one individually, so the Home became a screen they clicked through, not a place they returned to.

The bet: make the Home a single entry point that serves both jobs at once — first-time setup and ongoing performance — by adapting to who's actually looking at it.

My role

What I owned

I owned the end-to-end design of the new Home — from framing the activation-versus-retention tension, through two prototyped directions tested against new-user and active-customer personas, to the adaptive Home that shipped in Q2.

I defined the empty-state and populated-state treatments, and broke the Home into modular building blocks — a templates grid, a "Build it for me" entry, and performance widgets — so it could adapt per user and keep evolving after launch.

The solution

One Home that adapts to who's using it

Detect user state, then lead with the job that matters to them right now.

One Home, two states — not two pages

Adaptive

The obvious answer was a separate onboarding page and a separate dashboard. We didn't split it. One Home reads whether you have active bots yet: activation-focused when you don't, performance-focused when you do — so the same surface grows with the user instead of stranding them on the wrong page.

New users: proven templates and a "Build it for me" path

Activation

To kill the blank-wall problem, the empty state leads with industry-proven templates and a "Build it for me" entry that hands off straight into the builder. The goal is sign-up to a working first bot in minutes, not the paralysis of a blank canvas.

Returning users: performance at a glance

Retention

Once a user has live bots, the Home leads with what they came to check — sessions, leads, conversion, and top flows — surfaced up front instead of buried inside each individual bot. The Home becomes the reason to log in between editing sessions, not a screen to click past.

Modular building blocks

Systemized

Templates grid, "Build it for me" entry, and performance widgets are composable blocks, not a fixed layout. That let the Home adapt per user state today — and gives the team room to reorder, add, and test blocks as the product evolves.

Validating it

Two directions, tested before we committed

Rather than argue the Home in the abstract, I prototyped two directions and put them in front of the two personas they're meant to serve:

  • Home 1 — Templates-led — leads with a templates gallery to get new users building immediately.
  • Home 2 — Pills + checklist — leads with a guided, step-by-step start.

User testing against new-user and active-customer personas — plus an A/B test plan for the live rollout — pointed to a hybrid: an activation-first empty state for users with no bots, flipping to a performance-first state once bots go live. That became the v1 that shipped.

Results

More new users reach a real first chat

In a live experiment, accounts that saw the new Home completed the full activation journey — onboarding → publish a bot → first real conversation, within 14 days — 39.7% of the time, versus 33.4% for control. A 6.3-point lift, ~19% better.

  • +6.3 points end-to-end. Onboarding → first chat converts at 39.7% with the new Home vs. 33.4% for control — a ~19% relative improvement.
  • The biggest gain is at the last step — the real conversation. Of accounts that published a bot, 62% of New Home accounts went on to a first chat, vs. 54% for control. The Home doesn't just get bots published; it pushes users to activate them with live traffic.
  • It confirms the original problem. The old Home wasn't driving activation. Giving new users a clear path to a working bot moves the needle where it counts — real conversations, not just published drafts.

What I'd take forward

One page can serve two users — if it reads state first

The temptation was to build two things: an onboarding page and a dashboard. The stronger answer was one Home that knows where the user is in their journey and leads with the job that matters right then. Designing for state, not for an average user, is what let a single surface carry both activation and retention.
