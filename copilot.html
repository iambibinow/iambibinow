The bet

Designing Landbot's AI Assistant aka Copilot

Landbot's original AI was supposed to be the on-ramp for new builders. Instead it was a drop-off cliff.

New signups hit timeouts, cryptic errors, and no memory of the conversation they were having — right at the moment we most needed them to succeed. It ran on rigid workflows and tried to do everything, badly.

The company had set a hard target: grow accounts that reach 3+ real chats — the point where a bot starts delivering value — from 2,198 to 7,500 per quarter. Landbot AI was the lever. So the bet was to rebuild it as an LLM agent, and to make the experience trustworthy enough that people would actually let it build for them.

The solution

Solutions that shaped Copilot's functionalities

Monitor performance, increased value perception, and improved engagement.

A panel the user can move

Flexible / Familiar

Landbot AI's placement belongs to the user.

By default it docks to the right — the copilot convention users already recognize from other AI tools, so it's familiar the first time and stays clear of the left rail's focused, single-object work. But that default is a starting point, not a cage: the user always has the final say on where help lives.

Apply / Discard preview cards instead of direct execution

Competent

The fastest version would let Copilot act directly, with undo as the safety net. We didn't ship that.

Copilot lays out every change before it lands: the blocks added, the order they connect in, the full path from start to finish, allowing the user to make informed changes with reassurance.

A visible thought process instead of hidden reasoning

Transparent

Most chat interfaces hide reasoning behind a clean answer. We made the opposite call.

The streaming reasoning does three jobs at once: it explains the why behind each action, surfaces uncertainty before it becomes a mistake, and teaches users how Landbot AI reads their canvas. Power users collapse it; new users learn from it.

Per-bot conversation history — not one global log

Continuity

Landbot AI's value compounds across a session — by the third interaction it's seen the canvas and can respond in shorthand. Wiping that on every bot-switch would reset the relationship to zero.

We tied history to the work, not the user: each bot keeps its own context, and within a bot users spin up focused threads — one for an integration, one for a broken flow — instead of one sprawling log.

A feedback control on every response — a rating, plus room to say why

Accountable

Every Copilot response carries a lightweight thumbs up / down. One tap is near-zero friction — but a rating on its own never tells you what went wrong, so it's paired with an open field where users can explain, in their own words, what worked or what broke.

This is the raw signal that feeds the evaluation loop: it turns a silent bad experience into a labeled example the team can act on, and it tells users their frustration actually goes somewhere.

Persona & behavior

A platform feature with a voice

I led the persona and behavior work as a structured design proposal, anchored in two frameworks — Persona Design for Conversational Interfaces and Behavior Design — applied to Landbot's context.

Competent

Grounded in how the builder actually works. It doesn't guess, or hedge for the sake of hedging.

Efficient

Respects the user's time. Action over conversation when there's a choice; as long as it needs to be, no longer.

Transparent

Surfaces uncertainty and scope limits instead of papering over doubt with confident language.

Closing the loop

An evaluation system on real conversations

Shipping the design was the start. Knowing whether it was working — and improving it over time — needed a system, not a spot-check.

Copilot is non-deterministic: the same request can succeed one day and confuse the next. So the core design work wasn't tooling — it was defining what "good" even means. I translated the persona principles into a scoreable rubric across the two dimensions that drive engagement and satisfaction:

  • Task Success — did Landbot AI accomplish what the user asked? (goal clarity, completion, confirmation, resolution)
  • Friction — how much extra work did the user have to do? (clarification efficiency, loop avoidance, failure recovery, effort)

Each pattern maps straight back to a persona trait: the confirmation gap is Transparency, the clarification loops are Efficiency, the failure message is Competence breaking down. The rubric made the persona measurable — and gave the team a headline reliability metric to drive down over time: Critical rate, 10% today → target under 5% a quarter, run weekly on a fresh sample via Langfuse.

Results

Five months in

Copilot v2 reached users in late January 2026. This isn't a clean version's comparison — the rebuild changed too many things at once for that to be honest — but the metrics show whether the design is doing its job: getting users from intent to a working bot.

What I'd take forward

Trust in an AI product is earned

For an AI product, the design doesn't end at the interface. The error state mattered more than any happy-path screen; the confirmation the user never saw did more damage than a slow response. Defining and measuring what a good response is — and owning that definition — is how a designer stays responsible for the experience once the model, not the pixels, is doing the work.

Status: v2 is live and iterating. The evaluation rubric is validated on production data; the continuous Langfuse loop is a working draft moving toward weekly automated scoring.
